## Snapshot (TL;DR)

**What it is:** A term sheet summarizes the proposed material terms of an investment. It is the blueprint for the definitive documents, diligence, approvals, and closing. Most provisions are usually stated as non-binding, while selected ones — confidentiality, exclusivity or no-shop, expenses, access, governing law — may be binding. The actual wording and applicable law control. The term sheet is where the financing choice in [how startup funding works](/fundraising/how-startup-funding-works) becomes a specific bargain.

**Why it matters:** The term sheet allocates far more than valuation: post-round ownership, option-pool dilution, exit proceeds, board seats, investor veto rights, anti-dilution protection, founder vesting, pro rata rights, legal fees, exclusivity, and the conditions that must be met before funds arrive.

**Core formulas:**

`Post-money valuation = pre-money valuation + new primary investment`

`Price per share = pre-money valuation / pre-financing fully diluted shares`

`Investor proceeds (1x nonparticipating) = max(preference, as-converted % x distributable proceeds)`

**Founder rule:** Evaluate every term sheet in five columns — cash and runway, ownership and dilution, downside and exit distribution, governance and control, and closing risk and time. **The highest valuation can lose on the other four.**

## Is a signed term sheet a financing?

No. A signed term sheet records serious intent, but cash arrives only after satisfactory diligence, investor approval, negotiation of definitive documents, board and stockholder approvals, charter and securities filings, and satisfaction of closing conditions.

The NVCA maintains model U.S. financing documents that map to distinct functions:

| Document | What it governs |
| --- | --- |
| **Certificate of incorporation (charter)** | Preferred-stock rights: liquidation, dividends, anti-dilution, protective provisions |
| **Stock purchase agreement** | Issues the shares; representations, warranties, closing conditions |
| **Investors' rights agreement** | Information, registration, and preemptive rights |
| **Voting agreement** | Board composition and drag-along |
| **ROFR and co-sale agreement** | Restricts and coordinates certain transfers |

The term sheet should be specific enough that these documents *implement* the deal rather than reopen its central economics and control.

## Why do term sheets matter more than they look?

**Leverage inverts at signature.** Before signing, a founder may have several financing paths. After signing a binding no-shop, the company often cannot solicit alternatives while the investor conducts diligence — and runway keeps shrinking. Terms that looked secondary become hard to reopen.

**Headline valuation does not determine founder proceeds.** Two term sheets can state identical pre-money valuations and produce very different outcomes through option-pool timing, participating preferred, the liquidation multiple, cumulative dividends, seniority, redemption, tranche conditions, and anti-dilution.

**Governance outlasts the cash.** Board composition and protective provisions affect budgets, executive hiring, future financings, debt, pool increases, acquisitions, and charter amendments. An investor may own 20% and still hold approval rights over actions common holders would otherwise control. Percentage ownership, board control, and class vetoes are **three separate control systems** — check all three.

**Early terms set precedent.** Rights granted at [Series A](/fundraising/pre-seed-seed-series-a) are routinely requested by later investors or carried into a senior stack. A small concession compounds across Series B and C. Negotiate the architecture, not just the immediate dollar impact.

## What are the economic terms?

**Investment amount and security.** Identify round size, lead and participating investors, primary versus secondary proceeds, the preferred series, initial and later closings, and whether commitments are firm or conditional. *Secondary purchases give cash to selling holders, not the company — never count them in runway.*

**Pre-money valuation and price per share.** The term sheet must define **fully diluted capitalization**: does it include the existing unissued pool, the pool top-up, outstanding and promised options, warrants, SAFEs, and notes with interest? If that definition is unresolved, ownership is unresolved.

**Option pool.** State the target unallocated pool after closing, whether outstanding awards count, whether top-up shares sit in the pre-money, and the hiring plan that justifies the size. A pre-money top-up dilutes existing holders without touching the investor's negotiated percentage — see the worked model in [Dilution and Cap Tables](/fundraising/dilution-cap-tables).

**Liquidation preference.** Specify the multiple, participating or nonparticipating, any participation cap, seniority relative to existing preferred, dividend treatment, what counts as a liquidation event, and conversion mechanics. Covered in depth in [Liquidation Preferences](/fundraising/liquidation-preferences).

**Dividends.** Distinguish noncumulative dividends payable only if declared from cumulative or accruing dividends that increase the preference. An 8% cumulative dividend materially raises the exit hurdle over several years even when no cash is ever paid.

**Anti-dilution.** A future issuance below the current preferred price may trigger broad-based weighted-average, narrow-based weighted-average, or full-ratchet adjustment. The charter formula and its exceptions matter — ordinary employee grants, strategic issuances, and equipment leases are often excluded.

**Pro rata rights.** A right to invest *more cash* to maintain ownership — not protection from dilution without investment. Define eligibility threshold, covered issuances, calculation basis, notice period, transferability, and termination.

**Redemption.** A right to require the company to repurchase shares after a period, subject to law and available funds. Rarely exercised, but it creates leverage and a balance-sheet overhang.

## What are the control terms?

**Board composition.** Specify size, which seats are elected by preferred, common, or all holders, founder-designated seats, the independent seat, *who selects and approves the independent*, observer rights, and vacancy handling. A five-seat board described as "two founders, two investors, one independent" is not neutral if the investor holds effective veto over the independent.

**Protective provisions.** Preferred holders may require class approval to change preferred rights, create senior or pari passu securities, sell or liquidate the company, declare dividends, repurchase stock, take on debt above a threshold, change board size, or amend the charter. Apparently technical language — such as blocking creation of a new series — can function as **approval rights over your next financing**.

**Founder vesting and acceleration.** Review credit for already-earned vesting, the remaining schedule, cliff, repurchase price, termination treatment, single- versus double-trigger acceleration, and treatment of a role change. Resetting a founder's entire schedule transfers substantial economic leverage.

**Drag-along, ROFR, and co-sale.** A drag-along can require covered holders to support a qualifying sale — check the approving groups, minimum price conditions, escrow allocation, several versus joint liability, and whether holders receive the same form of consideration. ROFR and co-sale protect the cap table but reduce founder liquidity.

## What are the process and closing terms?

**Binding versus non-binding.** Do not rely on the label. Read exclusivity, confidentiality, expense reimbursement, access, publicity, governing law, and termination. Binding status is a legal conclusion drawn from the whole document and applicable law.

**Conditions to closing.** "Subject to satisfactory diligence" gives the investor meaningful discretion. Other common conditions: investment committee approval, minimum round size, syndicate participation, corporate approvals, IP assignments, founder agreements, no material adverse change, and required filings. **Clarify known issues before exclusivity, not after.**

**Exclusivity.** Match the no-shop period to diligence scope, document readiness, the investor's approval process, syndicate formation, and — critically — your runway. Include a clear expiration and avoid automatic extension without consent.

**Tranches and milestones.** For a tranched financing, define each tranche's amount and date, the objective milestone, the measurement source, who determines satisfaction, cure or waiver rights, consequences of partial achievement, and whether price or rights change. The NVCA's October 2025 model-document update added a framework for exactly this, including optional language converting an investor's preferred into common if it fails to fund — stripping its liquidation preference and protective rights on previously purchased shares.

## Worked example: same valuation, different exit economics

Assume a $20M pre-money, $5M new investment, $25M post-money, 20% investor as-converted ownership, and no debt or transaction costs (to isolate the preference).

- **Term Sheet A:** 1x nonparticipating preferred → `max($5M, 20% x exit)`
- **Term Sheet B:** 1x participating preferred, no cap → `$5M + 20% x (exit - $5M)`

| Exit equity proceeds | A: investor | B: investor | A: common | B: common |
| ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| $10 million | $5.0M | $6.0M | $5.0M | $4.0M |
| $18 million | $5.0M | $7.6M | $13.0M | $10.4M |
| $50 million | $10.0M | $14.0M | $40.0M | $36.0M |

The headline valuation and the 20% ownership are **identical**. Participation transfers an extra $1.0M, $2.6M, and $4.0M to the investor across the three scenarios.

**Conversion breakpoint under A.** The investor converts once 20% of proceeds exceeds the $5M preference: `$5M / 20% = $25M`. Below a $25M exit it takes the preference; above it, converting pays more.

**Caveats.** This model assumes a single preferred series, no debt, no transaction costs, no accrued dividends, and no option exercise proceeds — every one of which shifts the numbers in practice. The "common" column also lumps together founders, employees, and the unissued pool, so a founder's individual share is smaller than it appears. Real waterfalls stack multiple series with differing seniority; treat this as the shape of the effect, not a forecast.

## Key Facts

- **98.2% of Cooley's Q1 2026 reported financings carried a 1x liquidation preference**, and **96.4% used nonparticipating preferred** — structures favorable to companies. [Cooley, Q1 2026 Venture Financing Report](https://www.cooley.com/news/insight/2026/2026-04-29-q1-2026-venture-financing-report)
- **Redemption provisions rose from 1.8% to 6.1% of deals** between Q4 2025 and Q1 2026, while accruing dividends fell from 3.6% to 2.4%. [Cooley, Q1 2026](https://www.cooley.com/news/insight/2026/2026-04-29-q1-2026-venture-financing-report)
- **Cooley recorded 165 venture financings representing $39.9B in Q1 2026**, with deal volume at its lowest level since Q3 2016 and up rounds at 86%. [Cooley, Q1 2026](https://www.cooley.com/news/insight/2026/2026-04-29-q1-2026-venture-financing-report)
- **The NVCA updated five model documents on October 2, 2025** to add tranched-financing mechanics, including optional conversion of a non-funding investor's preferred into common. [NVCA](https://nvca.org/press_releases/nvca-releases-2025-updates-to-model-legal-documents/)

Market data is context, not entitlement. A term can still be harmful when it interacts with a small exit range, a concentrated board, or short runway.

## How do you compare competing term sheets?

| Dimension | Question |
| --- | --- |
| Cash and runway | How much primary cash arrives, when, under what conditions? |
| Dilution | What is each holder's fully diluted percentage after pool and convertibles? |
| Downside | What do investors and common receive across a range of exit values? |
| Control | Who controls the board, class votes, financing, sale, debt, budget? |
| Follow-on | Are pro rata, pay-to-play, and reserve expectations aligned? |
| Execution | Is the investor approved, funded, responsive, and able to close? |
| Signaling | Will this investor and structure help or hinder the next round? |

Convert non-price terms into scenarios and dollars wherever possible. Do not assign them arbitrary "valuation points."

## What are the most common term-sheet mistakes?

- **Negotiating valuation in isolation.** Pool timing, preferences, dividends, and control routinely outweigh a modest valuation difference.
- **Assuming "20% sold" equals 20% founder dilution.** A pre-money pool increase plus converting securities pushes existing-holder dilution meaningfully higher.
- **Treating 1x as automatically harmless.** Participation, seniority, cumulative dividends, and stacked rounds can make a 1x preference economically powerful.
- **Reading protective provisions as boilerplate.** Thresholds and class votes can hand one investor a veto over your next financing or a sale.
- **Signing a long no-shop with little runway.** If the investor delays or withdraws, you return to market weaker and later. Cap the period and date it.

## When does term-sheet analysis break down?

- **The investor lacks final approval.** A partner's indication may still need investment-committee sign-off. Understand the decision path before granting exclusivity.
- **The round depends on an incomplete syndicate.** If the lead funds only after others fill the round, you have a term sheet but no financeable closing. Define minimum amounts and first-close mechanics.
- **The company needs cash before documents can close.** A bridge SAFE or note can alter the agreed cap table and require investor consent. Coordinate it explicitly.
- **A tranche milestone is subjective.** The investor gains a de facto option to withhold capital. Use objective measurements, evidence standards, timing, cure rights, and dispute mechanics.
- **Existing securities require consent.** Preferred protective provisions, note amendments, SAFE side letters, or debt covenants may block or delay closing.

## Frequently asked questions

**Q:** Can I negotiate a term sheet without a lawyer?

**A:** You can, but it is rarely wise. The term sheet's economics get implemented in a charter and four agreements whose language determines what you actually agreed to. Experienced startup counsel typically pays for itself on one provision.

**Q:** Which terms should I fight hardest for?

**A:** Generally board composition, protective provisions, liquidation preference structure, option-pool timing and size, and founder vesting credit. These compound over time and across later rounds; a point of valuation usually does not.

**Q:** Is a non-binding term sheet really non-binding?

**A:** Partly. The economic terms typically are not enforceable, but exclusivity, confidentiality, and expense provisions usually are — and reputationally, reneging on agreed terms is costly for both sides. Read which clauses survive.

**Q:** Should I sign the highest valuation?

**A:** Not automatically. Model post-round ownership including the pool, and model exit proceeds across a realistic range. A lower valuation with a post-money pool and clean nonparticipating preferred often beats a higher one with a large pre-money pool and participation.

**Q:** How long should exclusivity run?

**A:** Long enough for genuine diligence, short enough to preserve options — commonly 30 to 60 days, scaled to the investor's process and your runway. Insist on a hard expiration rather than automatic renewal.
