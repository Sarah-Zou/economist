## Snapshot (TL;DR)

**What it is:** Pre-seed, seed, and Series A are conventional names for early startup financing rounds. Each usually corresponds to a shift in what investors are underwriting:

- **Pre-seed:** Can this team turn a credible insight into a product and initial market evidence?
- **Seed:** Is there real demand, and can the company find a repeatable path toward product-market fit?
- **Series A:** Is there enough evidence to invest in scaling a repeatable growth engine and organization?

These are not legal categories or universal revenue bands. The SEC notes that federal securities law does not assign a separate exemption to a friends-and-family, angel, seed, or Series A label—the issuer still needs a valid registration exemption or a registered offering.

**Why it matters:** A stage label changes investor expectations about traction, diligence, terms, board rights, and scaling plans. It does not change the company's underlying facts or its legal duties.

**Core rule:** Define the round by the risk the money will retire and the evidence it must produce—not by the amount you hope to raise.

**Key framework:**

`Unresolved risk → measurable milestone → cash required → suitable instrument → next financing or sustainability`

## What changes between pre-seed, seed, and Series A?

| Stage | Main question being financed | Evidence commonly available | Primary use of funds | Common instrument pattern |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Pre-seed** | Is the problem real, is the insight differentiated, and can the team build? | Founder-market fit, customer discovery, prototype, design partners | Build the first product, validate the problem, recruit the core team, test willingness to pay | SAFEs and convertible notes; occasionally a small priced round |
| **Seed** | Do customers adopt, pay, return, and refer—and can the team find a repeatable motion? | Usable product, pilots or revenue, engagement cohorts, early pricing and channel signals | Prove demand and retention, test go-to-market, establish unit-economics baselines | SAFEs, notes, or a priced seed preferred-stock round |
| **Series A** | Can the company scale a repeatable engine without destroying economics or quality? | Initial customer base, proof of concept, stronger retention and growth, credible operating plan | Scale sales, marketing, product, hiring, infrastructure, and governance | Usually a priced preferred-stock round with negotiated investor rights |

The table describes common patterns, not gates. The SEC's small-business glossary similarly describes a seed round as an early round used for product development and market research, and a Series A as supporting a company with an initial customer base and proof of concept, commonly in exchange for preferred stock.

## Why does the distinction matter to founders?

**It changes the burden of proof.** At pre-seed, a strong deck can rest on a sharp problem, a non-obvious insight, a credible team, and a plan for turning uncertainty into evidence. At seed, investors want to see what customers actually *do*: activation, usage, retention, paid pilots, or conversion. At Series A, the central claim is that something is worth scaling—so the deck needs evidence of repeatability such as cohort quality, pipeline conversion, gross margin, churn, or CAC payback.

**It changes the financing structure.** Early rounds often use convertible instruments because the company is hard to value and needs to close quickly. The SEC defines a convertible note as a loan that can convert into another security, and a SAFE as a promise of a future ownership interest upon specified triggers. A note is debt with interest and a maturity date; a SAFE generally is neither. Both create dilution when they convert. Institutional Series A financings are usually priced preferred-stock rounds that make valuation explicit but add a fuller package of economic, governance, and protective rights—as the NVCA model documents illustrate.

**It changes dilution and control.** Stage labels do not determine dilution, but instruments and deal terms do. A SAFE stack can defer cap-table impact until a priced round; a Series A can add a board seat, protective provisions, pro rata rights, a liquidation preference, and an option-pool increase. Founders should compare fully diluted post-closing ownership, not just the headline valuation.

## How should you define each stage?

**Pre-seed finances the first credible evidence.** The money should buy answers to existential questions: Is the problem urgent enough for a specific customer to act? Can the team build and deliver? Will a customer use it, pay, or commit meaningful time or data? A good pre-seed milestone is specific enough to falsify the plan—"ship a secure MVP and convert five of ten design partners into paid pilots," not "finish the product and grow."

**Seed finances evidence of demand and a path to repeatability.** The product exists in a usable form, and the company is learning whether adoption, retention, pricing, and distribution can support a real business. There is no universal seed revenue threshold; the right evidence follows the business model's main risk. Y Combinator's seed guidance recommends raising enough to reach the next fundable milestone and tying the amount to a believable operating plan—not treating seed as merely "money before Series A."

**Series A finances repeatable scaling.** The investor is no longer underwriting the possibility of finding a market; the question is whether more capital can scale a validated motion with acceptable economics. A useful test is a coherent evidence chain:

`Target customer → urgent problem → differentiated product → retained usage → paid demand → repeatable acquisition → viable unit economics`

Series A also marks a governance transition. A lead may negotiate board representation and rights over budgets, financings, and senior hiring. YC's standard Series A term-sheet guidance stresses that price is only one issue—board structure and non-standard economic terms can matter more to a founder's future control.

## How do you size a round around a risk-retiring milestone?

1. **Name the risk.** Write one sentence: "This round exists to prove **X**." If the sentence only describes hiring or spending, it is not yet a milestone.
2. **Define the proof.** Choose the smallest set of metrics that would change a rational financing decision, using cohorts and unit economics where possible. The metric must fit the motion (activation and retention for product-led software; win rate, sales cycle, and CAC payback for sales-led).
3. **Calculate the cash needed.** Use cash timing, not accounting profit:

   `Monthly net burn = cash operating outflows − cash operating inflows`

   `Funding need = (monthly net burn × months to milestone) + one-time milestone costs + cash buffer − current unrestricted cash − committed non-dilutive inflows`

4. **Map the instrument to the facts.** Use a SAFE only after modeling its conversion and cumulative dilution; a note only if you understand maturity, interest, and repayment risk; a priced round when size, investor requirements, or cap-table complexity justify full documents.
5. **Check the legal pathway.** The round name is irrelevant to securities-law compliance. Every offer and sale of securities in the U.S. must be registered or rely on an available exemption (Regulation D, Regulation Crowdfunding, Regulation A, and others each have different limits and rules). Use qualified counsel.

## What is the core financing math?

**Priced round.** In a simple priced equity round:

`Post-money valuation = pre-money valuation + new investment`

`New investor ownership = new investment / post-money valuation`

`Existing holder post-round % = existing holder pre-round % × (1 − new investor ownership %)`

This clean math can understate founder dilution if the pre-money capitalization already includes SAFE and note conversions, warrants, promised equity, or an option-pool increase.

**Post-money SAFE estimate.** When a post-money valuation cap applies:

`Estimated SAFE ownership = purchase amount / post-money valuation cap`

For several post-money SAFEs at different caps, estimate each and sum them. YC's post-money SAFE guide uses this logic and warns that actual conversion can deliver more shares if the priced-round valuation is below or close to the SAFE cap, and that the priced-round's new money further dilutes the converted SAFEs.

## Worked example: from pre-seed SAFEs to a Series A

Assume a B2B software company starts with founders holding 90% of the fully diluted capitalization and an employee pool at 10%. *(Figures are illustrative.)*

**1. Size the pre-seed round.** The company expects $35,000 monthly net burn, 15 months to ship and convert five design partners into paid pilots, $75,000 of one-time work, a $100,000 buffer, and $100,000 of current cash.

`Funding need = ($35,000 × 15) + $75,000 + $100,000 − $100,000 = $600,000`

It raises $600,000 on a post-money SAFE with a $6M cap: `$600,000 / $6,000,000 = 10%`.

**2. Raise seed after the milestone.** It ships, closes paid pilots, and raises $1.2M on a second post-money SAFE with a $12M cap: `$1,200,000 / $12,000,000 = 10%`. The two SAFE groups now represent ~20% before Series A; the pre-SAFE block is 80% (founders `90% × 80% = 72%`; pool `10% × 80% = 8%`).

**3. Close a Series A.** It raises $5M at a $20M pre-money valuation. Post-money is $25M, so new investors take `$5M / $25M = 20%`, diluting pre-A holders by 20%:

| Holder group | Approx. pre-A ownership | Approx. post-A ownership |
| --- | ---: | ---: |
| Founders | 72.0% | 57.6% |
| Existing employee pool / holders | 8.0% | 6.4% |
| Converted SAFE holders | 20.0% | 16.0% |
| Series A new investors | — | 20.0% |
| **Total** | **100.0%** | **100.0%** |

This is an educational simplification. A closing model must use the actual SAFE and note terms, conversion prices, fully diluted capitalization, option-pool increase, pro rata participation, and warrants. The lesson: a "20% Series A" does not mean founders lost only 20% across the whole financing journey.

## Key Facts

- **~3,000 U.S. startups raised over $2.3 billion in pre-seed** on Carta in Q1 2026 (the total was still climbing toward ~$2.9B as data landed). [Carta, State of Pre-Seed: Q1 2026](https://carta.com/data/state-of-pre-seed-q1-2026/)
- **Convertible notes fell to a record-low ~7% of pre-seed rounds and 8% of dollars** in Q1 2026; SAFEs are the default instrument. [Carta, State of Pre-Seed: Q1 2026](https://carta.com/data/state-of-pre-seed-q1-2026/)
- **More than 60% of Q1 2026 venture dollars on Carta went to AI companies**, distorting stage benchmarks toward a single sector. [Carta, State of Private Markets: Q1 2026](https://carta.com/data/state-of-private-markets-q1-2026/)
- **Foundational-model AI startups raised at a ~$300M median Series A valuation versus ~$55M for non-AI startups**—evidence that a single "Series A benchmark" is misleading. [Carta, State of Private Markets: Q1 2026](https://carta.com/data/state-of-private-markets-q1-2026/)

## What are the most common mistakes?

- **Treating the stage as a legal status.** "Pre-seed," "seed," and "Series A" do not select a securities exemption, permit general solicitation, or remove disclosure and anti-fraud duties. The instrument and offering pathway matter; the label does not.
- **Defining the stage by check size.** A $3M round can be pre-seed for a capital-intensive company, seed for software, or a bridge after a Series A. Amount alone does not tell you what risk is being financed.
- **Treating fundraising as the milestone.** A financing event is an input, not customer or enterprise value. The useful milestone is the measurable value created with the capital.
- **Ignoring cumulative SAFE dilution.** Each SAFE looks small alone; together, multiple caps, discounts, side letters, and pool changes can transfer far more ownership than expected.
- **Optimizing only for valuation.** Board structure, liquidation preference, option-pool treatment, and investor quality can matter more than a modest valuation difference.

## When does the stage framework break down?

- **Capital-intensive and regulated companies.** Biotech, climate, hardware, and regulated products can raise large rounds pre-revenue because technical, clinical, or regulatory proof is the relevant milestone. A software ARR threshold would misclassify them.
- **Sector concentration.** Current benchmarks can reflect capital concentration rather than universal quality—see the AI-versus-non-AI Series A valuation gap above. Different sectors are not comparable markets.
- **Bootstrapped or profitable businesses.** Revenue, prepayments, grants, or suitable debt may finance the plan with better founder economics. Venture stages are a funding path, not a maturity ladder every company must climb.
- **Bridge rounds and extensions.** A seed extension, SAFE bridge, or insider round may buy time without changing the company's true evidence stage. Renaming a round does not retire risk.
- **A closed or selective capital market.** Reaching a milestone does not guarantee financing; fund strategy, sentiment, and reserves can delay or prevent a round. Build enough runway to survive a slower process.

## Frequently asked questions

**Q:** Is there a revenue number that "makes" a company Series A?

**A:** No. Metrics vary by business model, sector, and market conditions. Series A is about evidence that a motion is repeatable and worth scaling, not a fixed ARR line. Know the relevant investor benchmarks, but build the case around your own evidence chain.

**Q:** Should I use a SAFE or a convertible note at pre-seed?

**A:** SAFEs are now the default early instrument and avoid the interest and maturity-date pressure of a note. A note may still fit when an investor specifically wants debt protections. Either way, model the conversion and cumulative dilution before signing.

**Q:** Can I skip a stage or raise them out of order?

**A:** Yes. Companies skip pre-seed, go straight to a priced seed, or never raise a Series A. The sequence is a convention, not a requirement—finance the risk in front of you.

**Q:** How much should I raise?

**A:** Enough to reach the next value-inflecting milestone plus a buffer for forecast error and fundraising time. Size the round from a cash schedule tied to a milestone, not from a target dilution or a headline number.

**Q:** Does a higher valuation always help founders?

**A:** Only if the company can grow into the expectations it creates. A high price raised on weak evidence can make the next round harder (down-round risk) and often comes with terms that matter more than the number itself.
