## Snapshot (TL;DR)

**What it is:** A startup term sheet is a summary of the proposed material terms of an investment. It is the blueprint for definitive legal documents, diligence, approvals, and closing. It usually states that most provisions are non-binding, while selected provisions—often confidentiality, exclusivity or no-shop, expenses, access, and governing law—may be binding. The actual wording and applicable law control.

**Why it matters:** The term sheet decides much more than valuation. It can allocate:

- post-round ownership;
- option-pool dilution;
- downside and exit proceeds;
- board seats;
- investor veto rights;
- anti-dilution protection;
- founder vesting;
- pro rata rights;
- legal fees;
- exclusivity; and
- conditions that must be satisfied before funds arrive.

**Core financing formulas:**

`Post-money valuation = pre-money valuation + new primary investment`

`New investor ownership = new investment / post-money valuation`

`Price per share = pre-money valuation / pre-financing fully diluted shares`

Those formulas do not capture liquidation preference or control.

**Core preference formula for 1x nonparticipating preferred:**

`Investor exit proceeds = max(liquidation preference, as-converted ownership x distributable equity proceeds)`

**Founder rule:** Evaluate every term sheet in five columns:

1. cash and runway;
2. ownership and dilution;
3. downside and exit distribution;
4. governance and control; and
5. closing risk and time.

The “highest valuation” can lose on the other four.

## Definition: a term sheet is a map, not the closing

A signed term sheet usually records a serious intent to proceed, but it is not the financing itself. Cash arrives only after:

- satisfactory diligence;
- investor approval;
- negotiation of definitive documents;
- board and stockholder approvals where required;
- charter and securities filings;
- signature and closing conditions; and
- funding.

The National Venture Capital Association maintains model U.S. financing documents intended to reduce transaction cost, establish norms, present alternative terms, and provide internally consistent documents. Its current set includes an updated certificate of incorporation, stock purchase agreement, investors' rights agreement, voting agreement, and right of first refusal and co-sale agreement. ([NVCA, Model Legal Documents](https://nvca.org/model-legal-documents/))

Wilson Sonsini maps those documents to their functions:

- the **charter** contains preferred-stock rights such as liquidation, dividend, anti-dilution, and protective provisions;
- the **stock purchase agreement** issues the shares and contains representations, warranties, and closing conditions;
- the **investors' rights agreement** contains information, registration, and preemptive rights;
- the **voting agreement** sets board composition and drag-along rights; and
- the **right of first refusal and co-sale agreement** restricts and coordinates certain transfers. ([Wilson Sonsini, Series A definitive agreements](https://ecvc.wsgr.com/faq/financing/preferred-stock/what-do-definitive-agreements-for-a-series-a-financing-look-like))

The term sheet should be specific enough that these documents implement the deal rather than reopen its central economics and control.

## Why term sheets matter to founders

### Negotiating leverage changes after signature

Before signing, the founder may have multiple financing paths. After signing a binding no-shop, the company may be unable to solicit alternatives while the selected investor conducts diligence. Runway keeps shrinking. Terms that look secondary can become difficult to reopen.

Cooley notes that exclusivity is commonly among the binding provisions and recommends keeping the period proportionate to a financing process; its practitioner guidance identifies valuation, liquidation preference, board composition, protective provisions, founder vesting, anti-dilution, and exclusivity as high-priority negotiation points. ([Cooley GO, “Negotiating Term Sheets”](https://www.cooleygo.com/negotiating-term-sheets/))

### Headline valuation does not determine founder proceeds

Two term sheets can state the same pre-money valuation but create different outcomes through:

- option-pool timing;
- participating preferred;
- liquidation multiple;
- cumulative dividends;
- seniority;
- redemption;
- tranche conditions; and
- anti-dilution.

The SEC defines a liquidation preference as a negotiated right to payment before other investors in a liquidation event, often expressed as a multiple such as 1x or 2x plus applicable unpaid dividends. ([SEC, Small Business Glossary](https://www.sec.gov/resources-small-businesses/glossary))

### Governance persists after the money is spent

Board composition and protective provisions affect:

- budgets and executive hiring;
- future financings;
- debt;
- option grants and pool increases;
- acquisitions and sale;
- dividends and redemptions;
- charter amendments; and
- changes to the board.

An investor may own 20% but hold approval rights over actions that common holders could otherwise approve. Percentage ownership, board control, and class vetoes are three different control systems.

### Series A terms create precedent

Rights granted in an early round are often requested by later investors or carried into a senior stack. A small concession can compound across Series B, C, and beyond. Negotiate the architecture, not only the immediate dollar impact.

### The term sheet exposes investor behavior

Negotiation is an early operating test. Founders should observe:

- whether the investor explains trade-offs;
- whether it changes agreed points;
- how quickly it responds;
- who has decision authority;
- how it handles diligence findings;
- whether references match the pitch; and
- how it treats other investors and the team.

The relationship continues after closing.

## The three-part term-sheet framework

## 1. Economics

### Investment amount and security

Identify:

- total round size;
- lead and participating investors;
- primary versus secondary proceeds;
- preferred-stock series;
- initial and later closings;
- reserves or tranches; and
- whether the investor commitment is firm, conditional, or targeted.

Secondary purchases give cash to selling holders, not the company. Do not include them in runway.

### Pre-money valuation and price per share

At a simple priced round:

`Post-money = pre-money + new primary cash`

`Investor ownership = new cash / post-money`

But the term sheet must define **fully diluted capitalization**. Does it include:

- existing unissued pool;
- pool top-up;
- outstanding and promised options;
- warrants;
- SAFEs;
- notes and interest; and
- other convertible securities?

If that definition is unresolved, ownership is unresolved.

### Option pool

State:

- target unallocated pool after closing;
- whether outstanding awards count;
- whether top-up shares are included pre-money;
- the hiring plan that supports the size; and
- treatment of grants made before closing.

A pre-money pool top-up dilutes existing holders, not the new investor's negotiated percentage. See the worked model in [Dilution and Cap Tables](/wiki/fundraising/dilution-cap-tables).

### Liquidation preference

The term sheet should specify:

- multiple, such as 1x;
- participating or nonparticipating;
- cap on participation, if any;
- seniority relative to existing preferred;
- treatment of dividends;
- what counts as a liquidation event; and
- conversion mechanics.

Current market evidence is useful but not a substitute for reading the document. In Cooley's Q1 2026 reported financings, 98.2% had a 1x liquidation preference and 96.4% used nonparticipating preferred. That describes its observed deal set, not a legal rule or entitlement. ([Cooley GO, Q1 2026 Venture Financing Report](https://www.cooleygo.com/data/))

### Dividends

Distinguish:

- noncumulative dividends payable only if declared;
- cumulative or accruing dividends that increase the preference;
- cash versus stock dividends; and
- dividend preference relative to common.

An 8% cumulative dividend can materially increase the exit hurdle over several years even without cash payments.

### Anti-dilution

Preferred stock often converts into common at an adjustable ratio. A future issuance below the current preferred price may trigger:

- broad-based weighted-average protection;
- narrow-based weighted-average protection; or
- full-ratchet protection.

The charter formula and exceptions matter. Ordinary employee grants, strategic issuances, equipment leases, and acquisitions may be excluded depending on the document.

### Pro rata and preemptive rights

These allow qualifying investors to buy in future rounds to maintain some or all of their ownership. Define:

- eligibility threshold;
- scope of covered issuances;
- calculation basis;
- notice and exercise period;
- transferability; and
- termination.

Pro rata is a right to invest more cash, not protection from dilution without investment.

### Redemption

Redemption can give preferred holders a right to require the company to repurchase shares after a period, subject to law and available funds. Even if rarely exercised, it can create leverage and a balance-sheet overhang.

## 2. Control and governance

### Board composition

Specify:

- board size;
- seats elected by preferred, common, or all holders;
- founder-designated seats;
- investor seat;
- independent seat;
- who selects and approves the independent;
- observer rights;
- committee rights; and
- what happens if a seat is vacant.

A five-seat board described as “two founders, two investors, one independent” is not neutral if the investor has effective veto over the independent.

### Protective provisions

Preferred holders may require class approval for actions such as:

- changing preferred rights;
- creating senior or pari passu securities;
- selling or liquidating the company;
- declaring dividends;
- repurchasing stock;
- taking debt above a threshold;
- changing board size;
- amending the charter; or
- altering the business materially.

Scope, thresholds, class voting, materiality, and sunset provisions matter. Cooley emphasizes that apparently technical veto language—such as blocking creation of a new series—can function as approval over a future financing. ([Cooley GO, “Negotiating Term Sheets”](https://www.cooleygo.com/negotiating-term-sheets/))

### Founder vesting and acceleration

Review:

- amount of already-earned vesting credit;
- remaining schedule;
- cliff;
- repurchase price;
- termination treatment;
- single- or double-trigger acceleration;
- change-of-control period; and
- treatment of a founder role change.

Resetting a founder's entire vesting schedule can transfer substantial economic leverage.

### Drag-along, right of first refusal, and co-sale

A drag-along can require covered holders to support a qualifying sale. Check:

- approving groups;
- minimum price or conditions;
- treatment of representations and liability;
- escrow allocation;
- several versus joint liability; and
- whether holders receive the same form and amount of consideration consistent with their rights.

ROFR and co-sale provisions regulate founder and other covered transfers. They can protect the cap table but reduce personal liquidity.

### Information and inspection rights

Define financial statements, budgets, cap tables, KPI reporting, inspection, confidentiality, competitors, and termination thresholds. The company needs a reporting process it can actually operate.

## 3. Process and closing risk

### Binding and non-binding provisions

Do not rely on the label “non-binding.” Read:

- exclusivity or no-shop;
- confidentiality;
- expense reimbursement;
- access and diligence;
- publicity;
- governing law;
- termination;
- standstill; and
- dispute provisions.

Binding status is a legal conclusion based on the entire document and applicable law.

### Conditions to closing

Typical conditions can include:

- satisfactory legal, financial, technical, security, regulatory, or commercial diligence;
- investment committee approval;
- minimum round size;
- lead or syndicate participation;
- execution of definitive documents;
- corporate approvals;
- IP assignments;
- employment or founder agreements;
- compliance fixes;
- no material adverse change; and
- receipt of legal opinions or filings.

“Subject to satisfactory diligence” gives the investor meaningful discretion. Clarify known issues before exclusivity.

### Legal fees and expenses

State:

- whose counsel drafts;
- the company-paid cap;
- whether payment is due only at closing;
- treatment if the investor withdraws;
- special diligence costs; and
- multiple investor counsel.

### Exclusivity

Match the no-shop period to:

- diligence scope;
- document readiness;
- investor approval process;
- syndicate formation;
- holidays; and
- company runway.

Include a clear expiration and avoid automatic extension without consent.

### Tranches and milestones

For a tranched financing, define:

- amount and date of each tranche;
- objective milestone;
- measurement source;
- who determines satisfaction;
- cure or waiver;
- consequences of partial achievement;
- investor funding obligation; and
- whether price or rights change.

The NVCA's 2025 model-document update added mechanics for time- or milestone-based funding, reflecting the need for internal consistency when capital is not delivered in one closing. ([NVCA, 2025 model-document update](https://nvca.org/press_releases/nvca-releases-2025-updates-to-model-legal-documents/))

## Worked example: the same valuation, different exit economics

Assume:

| Financing term | Amount |
| --- | ---: |
| Pre-money valuation | $20 million |
| New investment | $5 million |
| Post-money valuation | $25 million |
| Investor as-converted ownership | 20% |
| Debt and transaction costs | $0, solely to isolate the preference |

Compare:

- **Term Sheet A:** 1x nonparticipating preferred.
- **Term Sheet B:** 1x participating preferred with no participation cap.

### Term Sheet A: 1x nonparticipating

The investor chooses the greater of:

- $5 million preference; or
- 20% of exit proceeds.

`Investor proceeds = max($5 million, 20% x exit value)`

### Term Sheet B: 1x participating

The investor receives:

1. $5 million first; then
2. 20% of the remaining proceeds.

`Investor proceeds = $5 million + 20% x (exit value - $5 million)`

for this simplified example when proceeds exceed $5 million.

### Exit comparison

| Exit equity proceeds | A: 1x nonparticipating | B: 1x participating | Common under A | Common under B |
| ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| $10 million | $5.0 million | $6.0 million | $5.0 million | $4.0 million |
| $18 million | $5.0 million | $7.6 million | $13.0 million | $10.4 million |
| $50 million | $10.0 million | $14.0 million | $40.0 million | $36.0 million |

The headline valuation and 20% ownership are identical. The participating preference transfers an additional $1 million, $2.6 million, and $4 million to the investor in the three scenarios.

### Conversion breakpoint under nonparticipating preferred

The investor converts when 20% of proceeds exceeds $5 million:

`Breakpoint = $5 million / 20% = $25 million`

Below $25 million, the investor takes the 1x preference. Above it, conversion produces more.

This is why founders should negotiate and model the waterfall, not merely compare pre-money valuations.

## Evaluating competing term sheets

Use a decision table:

| Dimension | Questions |
| --- | --- |
| Cash and runway | How much primary cash arrives, when, and with what conditions? |
| Dilution | What is each holder's fully diluted percentage after pool and convertibles? |
| Downside | What do investors and common receive across exit values? |
| Control | Who controls the board, class votes, financing, sale, debt, and budget? |
| Follow-on | Are pro rata, pay-to-play, or reserve expectations aligned? |
| Execution | Is the investor approved, funded, responsive, and capable of closing? |
| Partner quality | Do references show constructive behavior in difficult situations? |
| Signaling | Will this investor and structure help or hinder the next round? |

Convert non-price terms to scenarios and dollars where possible. Do not assign them arbitrary “valuation points.”

## How founders, investors, and operators use term sheets

| Role | Practical use | Deliverable |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Founder | Choose capital and partner; negotiate priorities | Ranked issues list and decision model |
| Investor | Reserve economics, governance, and diligence path | Approved investment outline |
| Board | Evaluate financing alternatives and fiduciary trade-offs | Board materials and authorization |
| Finance | Model ownership, pool, runway, and exits | Pro forma cap table and waterfall |
| Legal | Translate business deal into consistent documents | Issues list and definitive agreements |
| People team | Plan pool and retention | Hiring grant forecast |
| Existing investors | Evaluate conversion, waivers, and participation | Consent and pro rata plan |

## Common mistakes and misinterpretations

### Negotiating valuation in isolation

Pool timing, preferences, dividends, and control can outweigh a modest valuation difference.

### Assuming “20% sold” equals 20% founder dilution

A pre-money option-pool increase and converting securities can make existing-holder dilution higher.

### Treating 1x as automatically harmless

Participation, seniority, cumulative dividends, and stacked rounds can make a 1x preference economically strong.

### Ignoring the exit breakpoint

Model when preferred takes its preference versus converts. A preference matters most in low and medium outcomes.

### Accepting an undefined independent director

Selection and removal rights determine who controls the swing seat.

### Reading protective provisions as boilerplate

Thresholds and class votes can give one investor a veto over future financing or sale.

### Accepting full-ratchet anti-dilution without modeling it

Full ratchet can transfer severe dilution to common and other holders in a down round.

### Resetting founder vesting casually

Vesting affects termination leverage, acquisition proceeds, and founder retention. Give credit for time and value already created where appropriate.

### Signing a long no-shop with little runway

If the investor delays or withdraws, the company may return to market in a weaker position.

### Treating the term sheet as funded cash

Continue managing runway through the expected closing date plus delay. A signed term sheet can still fail.

### Leaving known diligence problems for later

Missing IP assignments, inaccurate cap tables, unapproved grants, tax issues, security incidents, and customer-contract restrictions can reprice or stop the deal.

### Allowing the definitive documents to reopen the bargain

Keep an issues list mapping every term-sheet point to the implementing document. Escalate deviations immediately.

## When this breaks

### The investor lacks final approval

A partner indication may still require investment committee approval. Understand the exact decision path before exclusivity.

### The round depends on an incomplete syndicate

If the lead will invest only after others fill the round, the company may have a term sheet but no financeable closing. Define minimum and maximum amounts and first-close mechanics.

### Diligence changes the risk

Material customer churn, IP ownership, regulatory exposure, cap-table errors, or missed forecasts can justify a changed deal or withdrawal. Resolve known issues early.

### The company needs capital before documents can close

Bridge financing may be necessary, but a new SAFE or note can alter the agreed cap table and require investor consent. Coordinate it explicitly.

### A tranche milestone is subjective

The investor can gain a de facto option to withhold capital. Use objective measurements, evidence, timing, cure, and dispute mechanics.

### Market conditions move during exclusivity

Valuation or risk appetite can change. A shorter process and prepared data room reduce exposure.

### Existing securities require consent

Preferred protective provisions, note amendments, SAFE side letters, debt covenants, or pro rata rights may block or delay closing.

### The term sheet mixes jurisdictions

Entity law, securities regulation, tax, employment, foreign investment, and data rules can require different documents and approvals.

### The company optimizes for “market terms” instead of its actual risk

Market data is context. A term can still be harmful when it interacts with a small exit range, concentrated board, capital-intensive plan, or short runway.

## Founder negotiation checklist

Before requesting term sheets:

- Build the current and pro forma cap table.
- Reconcile all SAFEs, notes, warrants, grants, and promises.
- Calculate the cash required to the next milestone with a delay buffer.
- Prepare a clean data room and diligence issue list.
- Decide priorities, acceptable ranges, and walk-away points.
- Check investor references and decision authority.

Before signing:

- Confirm primary cash, round size, lead check, and closing path.
- Define pre-money, post-money, fully diluted capitalization, and convertibles.
- Model the option-pool top-up and hiring basis.
- Model liquidation proceeds across low, base, and high exits.
- Review multiple, participation, cap, seniority, and dividends.
- Review anti-dilution formula and exceptions.
- Map board seats, independent selection, observers, and committees.
- Map protective provisions and thresholds.
- Review founder vesting, repurchase, and acceleration.
- Review pro rata, information, ROFR, co-sale, and drag-along rights.
- Identify binding clauses.
- Limit and date exclusivity.
- Cap legal fees and define payment conditions.
- List every diligence and closing condition.
- Define objective tranche milestones, if any.
- Confirm investor and board approvals.
- Have qualified counsel review the actual language.

Before closing:

- Track term-sheet points into definitive documents.
- Update the cap table through the exact closing.
- Obtain required board, stockholder, and investor consents.
- Complete charter and securities filings.
- Verify IP, employment, tax, and equity records.
- Confirm wire instructions and funding amounts independently.
- Communicate only after closing is legally and financially complete.
